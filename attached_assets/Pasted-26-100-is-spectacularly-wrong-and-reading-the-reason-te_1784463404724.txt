26/100 is spectacularly wrong, and reading the reason-text tells you exactly what went wrong. Look at the specific complaint: "The core insight is that AA's efficacy might be explained by Jamesian psychology... The author simply juxtaposes two lists of claims and notes their similarity. There is no novel analysis, no bridging mechanism proposed, no critical evaluation of either text's claims." But the actual paper, which you and I have both read in full, is precisely a bridging mechanism proposal — the higher power as externalized ego-ideal/superego, cashed out through projection driven by the alcoholic's rationalization-corrupted introspection, generating internal unity via superego-compliance, with a specific distinction between faith and psychosis based on whether the delusion is foundational or a foil for non-delusional self-reunification. The engine didn't miss this because it's a bad reader; it missed it because it wasn't shown. It read the first page — the introduction, which by design only sets up the puzzle and previews the parallelism — and then hallucinated a verdict about the whole paper based on that opening. The 26 is the promissory-trap detector firing on a paper's introduction as if it were a whole paper. Introductions are supposed to look promissory. That's what they are.
So this is a real bug and a fixable one, and the fix is upstream of the rubric — it's about scope. Right now the app can't tell the difference between "this is a whole passage that fails to pay out" and "this is the first page of a longer document that hasn't paid out yet because paying out is chapters two through four's job." The strict rubric assumes the input is the entire artifact. Feed it a first page and it correctly notices the payouts haven't happened, then incorrectly concludes they never will. Two possible fixes for the agent: (a) detect when input appears to be an excerpt/first-page/introduction (table of contents present, "in this paper we will," section headers for unshown sections) and downweight the promissory-trap detector proportionally — an introduction that promises substantive moves should be scored on the substance of the promises, not on the absence of their fulfillment; or (b) simpler, just add a preprocessing check: if the text contains a table of contents referencing sections not present in the input, flag as "excerpt mode" and score the announced structure and setup rather than penalizing for missing content. Right now the same rubric that correctly gutted Mac's abstract is treating your first page as if it were Mac's abstract, when in fact it's the opening of an actual paper that does the work — the work is just on the pages you didn't feed it.
